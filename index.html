To begin:

  • Blood gets filtrated at the glomerulus.
  • The filtrate (urine) then flows down the tubular system of the nephron.

The tubular system wall is composed of two sides, separated by a layer of cells (the walls of the tubule).

As we continue following the filtrate along each segment of the tubular system, you will learn that the properties of the cells in the tubular wall change.

Aditionally, the tubular wall serves a divider for two spaces:

  • The lumen, where urine flows.
  • The basolateral aspect, where blood flows

The urine has a highly concentrated fluid. Since the glomerulus can't filter out ions and small molecules, so they all get carried into the urine. Makes sense, right?

Since our body doesn't want to waste these ions and small molecules, it tries to minimize the loss by pulling them through channels in the cells of the tubular wall.

Fun fact: About 60-80% of the free water gets resorbed in the first segment of the tubular system, the Proximal Convoluted Tubule.

The nephron uses concentration as the "force" to move these molecules from the urine into the blood. (Remember, they were highly concentrated in the urine).

The urine is full of sodium (Na+) ions. Most of the channels couple the transport of small molecules (like glucose or phosphate) to sodium, so that they can get carried into the blood together using sodium's high concentration as a sort of "fuel".

These are some of the channels that use sodium as fuel (a process called, symport):

  • The sodium-glucose transporter (SGLT-2).
  • The sodium-amino acid transporter.
  • The sodium-phosphate transporter.
  • The sodium-HCO3 trnasporter.

On the other hand, other channels use sodium's concentration to move stuff out of the cell as sodium is being pulled in, a process called antiport. Here's an example:

  • The sodium-H+ antiporter.
